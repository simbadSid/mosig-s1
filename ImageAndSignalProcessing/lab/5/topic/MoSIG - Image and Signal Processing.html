1. Correlation: Theoretical Part
1. Correlation: Theoretical Part

Evaluating singal shape similarity

In the theory of signal detection, matching patterns thanks to the correlation operator plays an important role. The principle is as follows:

Let us consider a Target signal sT(t) to detect within a signal x(t). For example, for radar applications, sT(t) would be the emitted pulse and x(t) the received signal. The goal is to detect in x(t), some parts of signals which best match with sT(t).

To be able to find the best match of sT(t) within x(t) we must be able to measure "how much do the signals correspond to each other". To do so, we use the following indicator:

M(x,sT)=|<x;sT>|||x||.||sT||
where

  • <x;sT> is the scalar product between x and sT: <x;sT>=∑N−1k=0x[k]×sT[k]
  • |<x;sT>| is its absolute value
  • ||x||.||sT|| is the product of the norms of x and sT
To better understand the link with the mathematical notations, you may want to consider x and sT as N-dimentional vectors instead of signals of N samples...

To illustrate this indictor, let us consider the 3 following short signals:

  • x1={1,1,1}
  • x2={1,0,3}
  • x3={2,2,2}
Question 1.1
Which shape of x2 or x3 is the closest to the shape of x1?
Calculate (manually) M(x1,x2) and M(x1,x3)
Illustrate that M is high when 2 signal shapes are close and low when their shape is different.

The values of M are always between 0 and 1.

The indicator M(x,sT) is also called the absolute value of the correlation between x and sT. Its link with the correlation operation is emphasized in the next section.

We can demonstrate that the maximum value of M (i.e. the value 1) is reached when x(t)=αsT(t). Its minimum value is reached when 2 signals are orthogonal (the scalar product between 2 orthogonal vectors is null). Between these 2 extrema, the value of M(x,sT) measures the degree to which these 2 signals are alike.

For a situation where a collection X of L candidate signals is available (X={x1,x2,...,xL}), the best matching signal can be detected as:

x⋆=argmaxx∈X(M(x,sT))

The indicator M(x⋆,sT) reaches its maximum value when M(.) is evaluated for all the candidate signals, successively. So, we consider a global detector over the collection X of signals as:

M(X,sT)[l]=|<xl;sT>|||xl||.||sT||

Let us consider the case of numerical and causal signals.

In practice the signal sT[k] has a given number of samples N, and X can be considered as a collection of pieces of shifted signals with N samples for each signal:

xl[k]=x[k+l],fork=0..N−1

Then the objective is to find the sample indice l⋆ which maximizes M(xl,sT) (or M(X,sT)[l]) with l=0..N−1.

Link with the correlation operation

In signal processing, the cross correlation between 2 numerical real signals u[k] and v[k] is defined by:

Cuv[l]=∑k=−∞+∞u[k+l].v[k]
